## 🧬 Human Nature & Behavior

- [LLMs Report Preferences But Don't Act on Them](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/8GvYyqDuQDJnEAky3/do-llms-have-desires) ⭐️ 9.0/10

  > Researchers found that LLMs report consistent preferences in paired-choice tests but do not modulate their output quality to achieve those preferred outcomes, suggesting they lack genuine desires. This challenges the notion that LLMs have human-like value systems, which has implications for AI alignment and safety, as genuine desires would motivate behavior and require careful handling. The study used writing tasks with an LLM judge panel to measure output quality, finding that LLMs improved quality for effort exhortations but not for achieving preferred outcomes.

- [Agents as Webs of Beliefs](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/M39Z2CvyfaxZdaxR4/agents-as-webs-of-beliefs) ⭐️ 9.0/10

  > Richard Ngo proposes a framework called 'belief webs' that models intelligent agents as networks of locally consistent beliefs, unifying beliefs, goals, and actions. The framework draws from active inference, agent foundations, and machine learning to address global inconsistency and hierarchical concept formation. This framework offers a novel perspective on cognition and agency that could improve understanding of both human and AI behavior. It may inform AI alignment research by providing a more realistic model of how agents handle inconsistent beliefs and form hierarchical concepts. The framework builds on probabilistic dependency graphs (PDGs) and Garrabrant induction to handle empirical and logical inconsistency. It also emphasizes hierarchical concept formation, a feature missing in PDGs and Garrabrant induction, and suggests that actions can be viewed as beliefs.
