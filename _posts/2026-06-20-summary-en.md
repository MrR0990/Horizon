## [Chinese Researchers Demo 4-Layer 3D 2T0C DRAM with 400s Retention](https://www.ithome.com/0/966/543.htm) ⭐️ 9.0/10

Researchers at the Institute of Microelectronics of the Chinese Academy of Sciences, in collaboration with Beijing Superstring Equipment Research Institute, have demonstrated the first 4-layer 3D 2T0C DRAM structure using IGZO transistors, achieving 400-second data retention and 3 bits per cell. The work has been accepted at the 2026 IEEE Symposium on VLSI Technology and Circuits (VLSI 2026). This breakthrough addresses the growing demand for high-capacity, high-bandwidth memory in AI and high-performance computing by enabling a single-step multi-layer 3D integration scheme for DRAM, potentially overcoming the limitations of traditional SRAM and off-chip DRAM. It represents a significant step toward practical 3D DRAM that can be integrated on logic chips. The new 3D DRAM employs a vertical word-line architecture and dual-gate 2T0C cell design, optimizing read margin, dual-gate read control stability, and manufacturing cost. The IGZO-based dual-gate transistors provide excellent device performance and stability, enabling both fast write speed and long data retention of 400 seconds, along with 3-bit-per-cell storage.

rss · IT之家 · Jun 20, 10:33

**Background**: Traditional DRAM uses a 1-transistor-1-capacitor (1T1C) cell, but the capacitor is difficult to scale in 3D. The 2T0C (two-transistor, zero-capacitor) architecture uses two IGZO thin-film transistors, leveraging the very low off-current of IGZO to store charge on the parasitic capacitance of the read transistor, eliminating the need for a storage capacitor. IGZO (indium-gallium-zinc-oxide) is an oxide semiconductor material known for its low leakage current, making it suitable for capacitor-less DRAM. Prior work by imec and others demonstrated planar 2T0C cells, but this is the first demonstration of a 4-layer stacked 3D structure.

## [Pan Jianwei Team Achieves Chip-Based Quantum Network Over 540 km](https://www.ithome.com/0/966/525.htm) ⭐️ 9.0/10

Pan Jianwei's team, in collaboration with multiple institutions, has built a chip-based quantum key distribution (QKD) network using the twin-field QKD (TF-QKD) protocol, achieving a secure key rate of 2.93 bps over 540 km of ultra-low-loss fiber. The results were published in Nature Photonics on June 19, 2026. This breakthrough demonstrates the feasibility of integrating quantum communication components onto photonic chips, significantly reducing system complexity and cost, which is a critical step toward practical, large-scale quantum networks for secure communications. The chip-based transmitter combines a self-injection-locked laser using a high-Q silicon nitride microring resonator (100 Hz linewidth) with a thin-film lithium niobate photonic integrated chip (25 GHz modulation bandwidth, 2.6 V half-wave voltage, 34 dB extinction ratio). The network employs a quantum leaf-spine architecture supporting flexible user connections.

rss · IT之家 · Jun 20, 07:51

**Background**: Quantum key distribution (QKD) allows two parties to generate a shared secret key with information-theoretic security. The twin-field QKD (TF-QKD) protocol overcomes the rate-distance limit of traditional QKD, enabling longer transmission distances. However, it requires extremely stable single-photon interference between two independent lasers, which has previously made system implementation complex and bulky.

## [UK scientists cancel laser noise in quantum sensors](https://www.ithome.com/0/966/546.htm) ⭐️ 8.0/10

Researchers from Imperial College London and collaborators have experimentally demonstrated a differential measurement technique that cancels laser phase noise in atomic interferometers, recovering signals even when single-device measurements are overwhelmed by noise. This breakthrough removes a major obstacle to building large-scale quantum sensors, enabling detection of faint signals from dark matter and early-universe gravitational waves, which could help solve mysteries like the formation of supermassive black holes. The team used two ultracold strontium-87 atom clouds as separate interferometers sharing the same ultrastable clock laser; by comparing their outputs, common laser noise was canceled, achieving measurement precision at the quantum limit. The results were published in Nature on June 17, 2026.

rss · IT之家 · Jun 20, 11:43

**Background**: Atomic interferometers use the wave nature of atoms to make ultra-precise measurements, but they rely on laser pulses that introduce phase noise much larger than the target signals. The differential measurement technique, long theorized, had not been validated under realistic conditions until now. This work is part of the UK's AION program, which aims to build long-baseline atom interferometers for fundamental physics.
